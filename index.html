When you think about how to get around in a city, what usually comes to mind is a map of streets and sidewalks forming islands -- city blocks -- of buildings, in a sea of car and pedestrian traffic, streets, and lanes.

But some of the most iconic and charming cities around the world are known for their alleyways.


Corniglia, Italy

Cordoba, Spain

Cairo, Egypt

New Haven is a small city, known for a lot of things — (A)pizza, Yale, Elm trees — but alleys are not usually one of them.

But New Haven's alleys, while lesser-known, form an entire navigable network of invisible paths, traversing and snaking through the city.


Merriam-Webster defines an alley as "a walk bordered by trees or bushes; a narrow street"

Elihu Rubin, Yale Professor of Urban Studies, describes them as "an urban morphological quality" often for access to garages and trash pickup.

But these places, which might not fit that definition, are all going to be considered alleys:


Alexander
Walk

Burgerway Alley

Pitkin Plaza

Note that the Alexander Walk will be considered an alley, but Wall St will not.

And while they might not be "tree-shrouded walks", have garage or trash access, or even be particularly narorw, the simple reason for their inclusion is that sometimes places just have the feel of an alley.

Oftentimes this "feeling" arises from the lack of focus on motor vehicle through-traffic -- none of the places that will be considered alleys have unrestricted car traffic -- which makes them quieter, more peaceful, and feel more human.

If I have time: Maybe go to all of the accessible alleys and measure average noise levels for a minute or two, and compare to sidewalks? Plot it all. Then, I can likely make the point that they're also islands of quiet in the city.

In line with this fuzzy definition, New Haven's Alleys come in many shapes and sizes.
Some of which are wide and can even fit cars and trucks,
others are narrow and might require a little shimmying to fit through,
and others still lie somewhere in between.

They're used in a lot of different ways, too, from just walking through, to shopping and dining, residential access, or even this one that... spreads religion? *label it on map "missionary alley"

*not its real name

And every so often, an alley can truly show itself off, become something truly exceptional, and blossom -- Like New Haven's Court Street, a quaint alley in the Wooster Square neighborhood, lined with townhouses. Usually quiet, on Sunday, April 14th, the alley was bustling with activity for New Haven's Cherry Blossom festival.

The various appearances, sizes, and "feels" of New Haven's many alleys leads to some being used more than others. We can visualize how frequently each alley is entered or travelled through.

But in almost every case, public alleys get far more use than private alleys.

Alleys add a lot of character to a city. And there are new alleys being built in New Haven -- but they're not public.

Many of the new housing developments in downtown New Haven have used what could be public alley space as private courtyard space.

These alley spaces could often be used as walking shortcuts, or be lovely paths to stroll or congregate;

But right now, almost all of these new alleys are private spaces with exclusive entry for their residents; and these developments are all expensive luxury apartments.

While the privatization of this space is the culmination of many aspects of modern urban development,

it's more important that we notice and recognize the value of our alleys.

Compared to the extensive street network, New Haven's little alleys are short, tight, and infrequent.

But they're cute, fun, and have a lot of potential. They're spaces that can shorten commutes, be enjoyable to walk through, and serve as a spot to congregate and chill. And given the frequency of their use, we know there is demand for them, and people enjoy the spaces.

And as is the case with the conversion of Wall Street to the Alexander Walk,

Or the redevelopment and alley-ification of Court St by the Orange Street Promenade,

We can make more quaint alleys that are lovely to walk through, with the right advocation and approach to urban development.

Our alleys should be as cherished, as known, and as prioritized as spaces as our streets and parks. One day, maybe New Haven can be full of alleys as versatile and as beautiful as Court St.
