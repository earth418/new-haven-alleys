When you think about how to get around in a city, what usually comes to mind is a map of streets and sidewalks forming islands -- city blocks -- of buildings, in a sea of car and pedestrian traffic, streets, and lanes.

But some of the most iconic and charming cities around the world are known for their alleyways.


Corniglia, Italy

Cordoba, Spain

Cairo, Egypt

New Haven is a small city, known for a lot of things — (A)pizza, Yale, Elm trees — but alleys are not usually one of them.

But New Haven's alleys, while lesser-known, form an entire navigable network of invisible paths, traversing and snaking through the city.


Merriam-Webster defines an alley as "a walk bordered by trees or bushes; a narrow street"

Elihu Rubin, Yale Professor of Urban Studies, describes them as "an urban morphological quality" often for access to garages and trash pickup.

But these places are all going to be considered alleys:


Wall St

Federal alley

Pitkin plaza

And while these might not be "tree-shrouded walks", have garage or trash access, or even be particularly narorw, the simple reason for their inclusion is that sometimes places just have the feel of an alley.

In line with this fuzzy definition, New Haven's Alleys come in many shapes and sizes, some of which are wide and can even fit cars and trucks, others require a little shimmying to fit through.

They're used in a lot of different ways, too, from walking through to shopping and dining, residential access, or even this one that... spreads religion? *label it on map "missionary alley*

*not its real name

And every so often, an alley can truly show itself off, become something truly exceptional, and blossom -- Like New Haven's very own Court Street, a quaint alley in Wooster Square bounded by townhouses. Usually quiet, on Sunday, April 14th, the alley was bustling with activity for New Haven's Cherry Blossom festival.

All the differences between New Haven's alleyways leads to some being used more than others. This is particuarly noticable when looking at alleys that lead from street to street or street to parking lot...

And charting how frequently each alley is entered or travelled through.

But in almost every case, public alleys get far more use than private alleys.

Alleys add a lot of character to a city. And there are new alleys being built in New Haven -- but they're not public.

Many of the new housing developments in downtown New Haven have used what could be public alley space as private courtyard space.

These alley spaces could often be used as walking shortcuts, or be lovely paths to stroll or congregate;

But right now, just about all of these new alleys are private spaces with exclusive entry for their residents; and these developments are all expensive luxury apartments.

New Haven's little alleys are short, tight, and infrequent, especially compared to the extensive street network.

But they're cute, fun, and have a lot of potential. They're spaces that can shorten commutes, , and be a place to congregate.
